​1. Kitchen Knives

Knives are a must-have in any kitchen. Knowing about different types, what they're made of, how to keep them in good shape, and what they're for can make cooking safer, quicker, and way more fun.

A. Types of Kitchen Knives

There's a knife for just about everything:

Chef’s Knife

Size: Usually 8–10 inches long.

Good for: Doing it all—chopping, slicing, dicing, and mincing veggies, fruits, meat, and herbs.

Paring Knife

Size: 3–4 inches long.

Good for: Peeling, trimming, and small work like cleaning shrimp or cutting up citrus.

Utility Knife

Size: 5–7 inches long.

Good for: Something in between a chef’s knife and a paring knife; works for slicing sandwiches, small veggies, or cheese.

Bread Knife

Blade: Has teeth like a saw (serrated).

Good for: Cutting bread or soft fruits without squishing them.

Carving/Slicing Knife

Size: 8–15 inches long.

Good for: Slicing roasts, chicken, or ham; the long, thin blade stops tearing.

Boning Knife

Blade: Thin and bendy.

Good for: Taking bones out of meat, chicken, and fish.

Cleaver

Blade: Big, square, and heavy.

Good for: Chopping bones, crushing garlic, or super tough chopping jobs.

Santoku Knife

From: Japan.

Good for: Like a chef’s knife but great at slicing, dicing, and mincing. Often has little dents (Granton edge) so food doesn't stick.

B. What Knives Are Made Of

What a knife is made of changes how sharp it gets, how long it lasts, and how much work it takes to care for it:

Stainless Steel

Doesn't rust.

Needs sharpening every so often.

Cheap and easy to care for.

High-Carbon Steel

Stays sharp longer.

Can rust; needs to be dried and oiled now and then.

Ceramic

Super sharp and light.

Breaks easy; can chip if you drop it.

Needs special tools to sharpen.

C. Knife Handles

Wood: Feels good in your hand, but you have to take care of it so it doesn't crack or get moldy.

Plastic: Easy to clean, doesn't get ruined by water.

Composite: Lasts long, feels good, and water doesn't hurt it.

Metal: Very strong, can be slippery; used a lot for pro knives.

D. Taking Care of Knives

Sharpening

Use a sharpening stone, steel, or get it done by someone who knows how.

Steel keeps the edge straight.

Cleaning

Wash knives by hand right after you use them.

Don't use dishwashers (the heat and soap mess up the blade and handle).

Where to Keep Them

Knife block, magnetic strip, or cover.

Don't leave them loose in a drawer or they'll get dull and you might cut yourself.

2. Cutting Boards

Cutting boards do more than just give you a place to cut—they keep your knives from getting wrecked and stop germs from spreading. What they're made of really matters.

A. Types of Cutting Boards

Wooden Boards

Types: Maple, walnut, beech, bamboo.

Good: Easy on knives, kills germs naturally.

Bad: Needs oiling and drying; can get bent if you soak it.

Works best for: Veggies, fruits, bread, cooked meats.

Bamboo Boards

Harder than wood; good for the earth.

Can make knives dull faster than soft wood.

Plastic Boards

Good: Light, you can put them in the dishwasher, don't stain easy.

Bad: Scratches easy; germs can hide in the scratches.

Works best for: Raw meat, chicken, and fish (easy to clean).

Composite Boards

Last a long time, dishwasher-safe, good for knives.

Doesn't get wrecked by heat; often looks like wood.

Glass Boards

Good: Doesn't soak up stuff, easy to clean.

Bad: Makes knives dull fast, can break.

Marble Boards

Mostly for rolling dough or serving food.

Super hard; not for chopping.

B. Cutting Board Care

Wood/Bamboo

Wash by hand with light soap.

Dry right away; don't let it sit in water.

Oil it now and then with food-safe mineral oil.

Plastic/Composite

You can put it in the dishwasher (but check what the maker says).

Get a new one if it gets deep scratches.

All Boards

Clean surfaces that touched raw meat with vinegar or weak bleach.

Don't cut on counters or you'll mess up your knives and the counters.

C. Best Knives for Each Board

Wood/Bamboo Boards: Good for most knives; keeps them sharp.

Plastic Boards: Great for meat to stop germs from spreading.

Glass/Stone: Just for light stuff or serving; don't chop hard on them.

D. Stay Safe

Keep knives sharp: dull knives are more likely to cut you.

Keep cutting boards still with a wet cloth or silicone mat.

Cut away from yourself.

Wash your hands and surfaces after touching raw meat.

3. Stuff that also matters

Have different boards for meat and veggies so germs don't spread.

Use the right knife for the job: don’t use a chef’s knife to peel small fruits.

Keep your knives clean, dry, and put away right.

Think about comfort: handles that feel good keep you from getting tired and having accidents.
